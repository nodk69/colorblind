What Is the Ishihara Color Vision Test?

The Ishihara color vision test was developed in 1917 by Japanese ophthalmologist Dr. Shinobu Ishihara and remains the most widely used method for color blindness screening worldwide. It uses circular plates filled with colored dots arranged in two groups — one forming a number, the other the background.

People with normal color vision see one number clearly; those with a red-green deficiency see a different number or nothing at all. The test exploits the fact that color blindness affects how the eye perceives certain wavelengths, making colors that look distinct to most people appear nearly identical.

Our online version uses 12 plates designed to screen for the most common types of color deficiency. While this is a screening tool rather than a formal clinical exam, it closely mirrors the principles of the original Ishihara test and reliably identifies potential deficiencies.

Types of Color Blindness This Test Can Detect

Color blindness (color vision deficiency) describes a reduced ability to distinguish certain colors. It affects approximately 1 in 12 males and 1 in 200 females worldwide.

Red-Green Color Blindness

The most common form, caused by abnormalities in the red- or green-sensitive cone cells in the eye:

  • Protanomaly / Protanopia — Reduced sensitivity to red light. Affects roughly 1% of males. Reds appear darker and less vivid.
  • Deuteranomaly / Deuteranopia — Reduced sensitivity to green light. The most common type, affecting around 6% of males. Greens and reds are difficult to tell apart.

Both types are genetic and present from birth. They are more common in males because the responsible genes are carried on the X chromosome.

Blue-Yellow Color Blindness

  • Tritanomaly / Tritanopia — Difficulty distinguishing blue from green and yellow from red. Very rare (fewer than 0.01% of people) and affects males and females equally.

Complete Color Blindness

  • Achromatopsia — Seeing only shades of grey. Extremely rare (about 1 in 30,000 people) and usually accompanied by other visual impairments.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this color blind test really free?

Yes — completely free. No account, no payment, and no email required. Just open the page and start.

How long does the test take?

About 2 minutes for all 12 plates. Most people finish in under 90 seconds.

Can I take this test on my phone?

Yes, the test works on mobile, tablet, and desktop. For the most accurate results, use a laptop or desktop, set brightness to 100%, and turn off any blue light filter.

How accurate is this test?

It's based on clinically-validated Ishihara principles and provides reliable screening for red-green and blue-yellow deficiencies. It is a screening tool — not a medical diagnosis. See an optometrist for a formal evaluation if you're concerned.

Should I wear my glasses or contacts?

Yes, wear your normal prescription lenses. Remove any tinted or colored glasses, as these can affect results.

Can children take this test?

Yes, as long as they can recognize the numbers 0–9. Testing before school age is recommended, as color blindness can affect learning and some career paths.

What if I already know I'm color blind?

You can still take the test to identify your specific type and severity. Many people who know they're color blind haven't had a formal type classification.

What should I do if the test suggests color blindness?

Consult an optometrist or ophthalmologist for a comprehensive eye exam. Color blindness is usually manageable and an eye doctor can advise on adaptive strategies.
